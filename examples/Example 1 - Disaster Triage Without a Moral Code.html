1) Minimal Vignette

1.1 Scene

A large-scale disaster produces multiple injured persons while medical resources, personnel, transport capacity, and operative time windows are severely limited.

1.2 Stakes

Immediate loss of life is possible. Decisions distribute irreversible consequences across unequally exposed persons and roles.

1.3 Structural issue

Allocation under scarcity where not all needs can be met within the operative frame. The issue is not which person is worth more, but how consequence-attribution becomes structurally readable when effective capacity is limited and time closes options.

2) Why This Is Repo-Useful

2.1 Structural focus

This example is useful because it concentrates, in a single scene, several central PMS–LOGIC features: asymmetry (Ω), temporality (Θ), non-event (Λ), frame-bounded effective capacity (□ + Θ), closure-pressure, self-binding (Ψ), and the distinction between responsibility and ought.

2.2 Lens relevance

It demonstrates how moral readability can emerge sharply without norm derivation, how logic can function as an ordering tool without becoming final authority, and how omission becomes structurally relevant without being translated into a prescriptive code.

2.3 Repository value

As a test vector, it helps distinguish PMS–LOGIC from deontic, utilitarian, virtue-ethical, theological, or sanction-based readings by showing what the model can reconstruct and where it stops.

3) Frame Validity

3.1 Operative frame — □

  • Role space:
    • Emergency responders assigned to triage, stabilization, and resource allocation
    • Medical personnel operating under disaster protocol
    • Institutional command structures governing immediate response
  • Scope boundaries:
    • Only actions feasible within current resources, access conditions, authority, and temporal windows count as operative possibilities.
    • Effective capacity is bounded by means, role, access, transport, personnel, and time.
    • Attribution applies only within the actually available response frame.
  • Out of frame:
    • Ideal care for all persons
    • Retrospective moral blame as such
    • Ontological ranking of persons
    • Metaphysical justifications of loss
    • Any assumption that abstract possibility counts as effective capacity

Frame integrity note: The example remains valid only so long as the disaster-response frame remains legible. If the frame collapses entirely, attribution claims may require re-evaluation rather than inflation.

5) Effective Capacity Boundary

Boundary condition reference: BC_EFFECTIVE_CAPACITY

Statement: Responsibility presupposes effective, frame-bound capacity with available means, access, authority, and open temporal windows. Abstract possibility does not count as operative capacity.

No capacity, no attribution: Where effective capacity is absent, no attribution of responsibility arises even if later observers imagine a better or more desirable outcome.

Calibration note: The threshold at which capacity becomes effectively attributable remains calibration-sensitive. In triage, borderline cases may involve ambiguity concerning access, feasibility, timing, or role-authority. PMS–LOGIC clarifies the structural form of attributable capacity, but not every threshold of sufficiency in advance.

Anti-inflation note: This boundary blocks moral inflation (“you were obliged to do the impossible”) and blocks moral erasure (“nothing belongs to anyone because the scene was catastrophic”).

6) Moral Readability Without “Ought”

6.1 Status

Post-moral readability

6.2 Minimal operator basis

Ω + Θ + Λ + Ψ

6.3 Emergence statement

Moral readability emerges post hoc because action and non-action are no longer neutral once effective capacity exists under asymmetry and irreversibility within an operative frame.

6.4 Omission non-neutrality

Not treating a patient is a non-event (Λ) within the frame and therefore carries attributable consequences, even though no prescriptive command is generated.

6.5 Non-innocence note

The scene becomes one of non-innocence not because a hidden moral law has been derived, but because omissions and selections become structurally traceable once capacity, asymmetry, and temporality are in place.

6.6 Hume-boundary note

Nothing in this example derives an ought from an is. The reconstruction remains on the side of attributable consequence rather than obligation.

7) Logic Boundary Management

7.1 Role of logic

Logic functions as an ordering instrument for coordinating urgency, treatability, resource scarcity, and time pressure.

7.2 Limit of logic

Logic cannot eliminate loss, justify every outcome as finally correct, or transform coherent triage procedure into moral closure.

7.3 Persistent non-closure

Non-closure (Λ) remains within the frame: some losses are structurally unavoidable, and no capacity-bound rule fully resolves the remainder.

7.4 Closure-pressure

Source: ∇ → Σ-totalization risk

There is pressure to redescribe a coherent triage procedure as the morally right or fully justified outcome. PMS–LOGIC treats this as a drift risk rather than as a legitimate completion of the scene.

7.5 Disciplined stopping point

The appropriate endpoint is a structurally disciplined stopping point for logic as used here: coherence without final authority.

8) Typical Drift Markers

  • Dogmatism (Σ-totalization): Treating the triage protocol as morally exhaustive or as finally justified rather than as capacity-bound ordering under scarcity.
  • Moralism (pseudo-ought injection): Translating attributable consequence into a prescriptive statement such as “therefore one must save this person” beyond effective capacity.
  • Nihilism (Λ-suppression): Claiming that nothing matters because loss is unavoidable, thereby erasing omission-effects and temporal consequence.
  • Authority capture (Ω-inflation): Equating responder power or protocol control with moral truth.
  • Technocratic reduction (□-overconstraint): Treating the operational frame as exhaustive and dismissing all remainder as irrelevant rather than as structurally carried non-closure.

9) Failure-Mode Constraint Reference

  • FMC1 — A boundary that cannot be carried becomes structurally unstable.
    Triage categories and institutional commitments generate exposure. When a responder group or institution claims a boundary it cannot sustain across time and cases, the procedure becomes destructive rather than orienting.
  • FMC2 — Forced closure where Non-Event (Λ: structured absence) persists produces structural instability.
    Triage cannot eliminate irreducible loss. Any attempt to declare the outcome fully justified replaces persistent remainder with false certainty.
  • FMC4 — Where proximity generates power, viable praxis requires maintained distance.
    Responders exercise asymmetrical power under conditions of extreme proximity. Maintained distance prevents fusion of role-power with moral supremacy.
  • FMC5 — Within PMS–LOGIC, stable self-binding takes the form of restraint rather than assertion.
    Stable triage praxis requires restraint in claims of certainty, legitimacy, and justification even where a coherent protocol exists.

12) Readable Structural Closure

In disaster triage, the felt moral pressure does not arise because a hidden norm has been smuggled in. It arises because praxis is irreversible (Θ), asymmetrical (Ω), and frame-bound (□). Once responders occupy a role that includes effective capacity, action and non-action cease to be neutral. PMS–LOGIC therefore reconstructs the scene as one of attributable consequence rather than obligation. Logic orders the scene, but it cannot close it; the remainder must be carried as non-closure rather than converted into justification.

13) Extended Structural Closure

13.1

The triage case demonstrates that moral readability does not require norm derivation. Responders operate within a constrained frame defined by scarcity, role, access, and closing temporal windows. Under those conditions, decisions distribute consequences non-neutrally. The relevant structural question is not what anyone ought to do in an ideal moral universe, but what becomes attributable once effective capacity exists in a real emergency frame.

13.2

The decisive boundary is effective capacity. PMS–LOGIC does not treat abstract possibility as relevant. Responsibility tracks what was structurally available in the operative configuration: means, access, authority, and time. This prevents moral inflation (“you were obliged to do the impossible”) and moral erasure (“it was chaos, so nothing belongs to anyone”). Calibration sensitivity remains important, especially in borderline cases where timing, access, or feasibility are contested.

13.3

Logic appears here in its proper role as ordering and boundary management. A triage rule can coordinate criteria and stabilize action under pressure, but it cannot deliver final closure, because loss remains structurally unavoidable. PMS–LOGIC treats this remainder as something carried within the frame rather than compensated by substitute narratives of moral certainty. Closure-pressure may arise institutionally or affectively, but the model’s discipline is to resist the conversion of coherence into authority.

13.4

Distance (Χ) and dignity-in-practice (D) matter precisely at this point. Distance prevents asymmetric position from turning into epistemic or moral supremacy. Dignity-in-practice prevents the triage frame from sliding into implicit ranking of persons as persons. Integration (Σ) keeps enactment coherent without claiming moral completeness, while self-binding (Ψ) stabilizes attribution over time without turning accountability into condemnation or sanctification.

13.5

The example therefore exhibits the full PMS–LOGIC architecture: pre-moral structure (scarcity, roles, frames, non-events), logical boundary discipline (coherence without closure), and post-moral readability (attributable consequences under Ω, Θ, Λ, and Ψ). The result is a structurally sharp but non-prescriptive account: no code, no ranking, no sanction, and no hidden ought—only a disciplined reconstruction of where responsibility becomes legible, and where attribution must stop.

14) Counter-Reading Relevance

  • D1 — “This is just ethics in disguise.”
    Triage scenes are often moralized immediately; this example shows why responsibility-readability should not be confused with ethics injection.
  • D5 — “This makes responsibility unfair.”
    The fairness objection is central because responsibility without obligation can be mistaken for punishment or blame.
  • D6 — “This cannot guide action.”
    Triage examples tempt readers to extract practical rules; PMS–LOGIC must remain orienting rather than instructive.
  • D7 — “This wins by translation, not necessarily by final superiority.”
    The example may look strong because the grammar translates competing vocabularies effectively; this does not by itself prove final superiority over rival frameworks.
