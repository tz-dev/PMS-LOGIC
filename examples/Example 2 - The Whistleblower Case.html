1) Minimal Vignette

1.1 Scene

An employee becomes aware of serious internal misconduct within an organization and faces the possibility of reporting, escalating, delaying, or remaining silent within existing institutional channels.

1.2 Stakes

Harm to third parties may continue, institutional integrity may degrade, and the employee may face retaliation, exposure, career loss, or legal risk. Consequences unfold across unequal positions and over irreversible time.

1.3 Structural issue

The issue is whether omission (non-reporting) within a role-bound and channel-bound frame becomes attributable under asymmetry and temporality, without deriving a duty to disclose.

2) Why This Is Repo-Useful

2.1 Structural focus

This example is useful because it concentrates several central PMS–LOGIC features in an institutional setting: informational asymmetry, role-bounded capacity, channel-conditioned omission, temporal delay, retaliation risk, attractors of silence, and pressure to convert attribution into moral command.

2.2 Lens relevance

It demonstrates how silence can become a structurally relevant non-event (Λ), how responsibility can become attributable without heroization or condemnation, and how logic must remain an ordering tool rather than a rule-producing authority.

2.3 Repository value

As a test vector, it helps distinguish PMS–LOGIC from deontic duty models, virtue-ethical hero narratives, institutional apologetics, and nihilistic evasions by showing what becomes structurally legible once information, asymmetry, time, and self-binding interact.

3) Frame Validity

3.1 Operative frame — □

  • Role space:
    • Employee operating under contractual, legal, and procedural constraints
    • Managers, compliance officers, ombudspersons, or legal channels within the organization
    • Institutional actors who control reporting pathways, documentation, escalation, and retaliation exposure
  • Scope boundaries:
    • Only reporting, escalation, or disclosure options available within actual channels, access conditions, authority structure, and time windows count as operative possibilities.
    • Effective capacity is bounded by access to evidence, reporting channels, role-position, exposure conditions, and temporal availability.
    • Attribution applies only within the actually available organizational frame.
  • Out of frame:
    • Heroic expectations detached from operative capacity
    • Retrospective purity tests
    • Global judgments about personal character
    • Assumptions that institutional policy guarantees safety, fairness, or justice
    • Any equation of abstract speakability with effective reporting capacity

Frame integrity note: The example remains valid only so long as the institutional frame is sufficiently legible to identify roles, channels, and temporal windows. If channels are fictive, inaccessible, or retaliatory beyond operative use, attribution claims may require recalibration rather than inflation.

    • Failure to report within a known reporting window

    • Deferred action under known institutional risk
    • No institutional frame can guarantee safe, fair, or complete resolution
  • Α (Attractor):
    • Organizational norms that normalize silence or delay
    • Recurrent deferral to “someone else will act.”

    • Routine dissipation of responsibility through bureaucratic delay
  • Ω (Asymmetry):
    • Exposure gradients:
      • The organization may hold power over employment, reputation, and access

      • Reporting capacity is role-bound, evidence-bound, and channel-bound
      • Risk exposure to retaliation may be significantly asymmetric
      • Institutional power may distort which disclosures are structurally feasible

    • Delayed reporting can increase harm
    • Remedial options may narrow or disappear over time

    • Shift from “moral courage” to “capacity-bounded disclosure under institutional asymmetry.”

    • Hold back from valorization or condemnation
    • Prevent institutional power from defining truth or innocence by default
    • Prevent disclosure from being romanticized as universally mandatory
    • Keep the reading structural rather than theatrical or exemplary
  • Σ (Integration):
    • Coordinate channel availability, retaliation risk, evidentiary access, and temporal urgency
    • Preserve structural coherence without collapsing into a universal reporting rule
    • Integrate silence, risk, and consequence without producing total closure

    • Attribution remains possible without converting the actor into hero, traitor, or moral exemplar

5) Effective Capacity Boundary

Boundary condition reference: BC_EFFECTIVE_CAPACITY

Statement: Responsibility presupposes effective, frame-bound capacity with available channels, sufficient access, role-relevant authority, and open temporal windows. Abstract speakability does not count as operative reporting capacity.

No capacity, no attribution: Where effective capacity is absent, attribution of responsibility does not arise even if later observers imagine that disclosure would have been preferable or admirable.

Calibration note: The threshold at which disclosure capacity becomes effectively attributable remains calibration-sensitive. Borderline cases may involve inaccessible channels, evidentiary uncertainty, extreme retaliation exposure, unclear role-authority, or temporally degraded intervention windows. PMS–LOGIC clarifies the structural form of attributable capacity, but not every threshold of sufficiency in advance.

Anti-inflation note: This boundary blocks moral inflation (“you were obliged to sacrifice yourself regardless of capacity”) and blocks moral erasure (“nothing is attributable because institutional outcomes were uncertain”).

6.1 Status

Post-moral readability

6.2 Minimal operator basis

Ω + Θ + Λ + Ψ

6.3 Emergence statement

Moral readability emerges post hoc because silence and disclosure cease to be neutral once information is held within an operative frame under asymmetrical power and irreversible time.

6.4 Omission non-neutrality

Silence functions as a non-event (Λ) within the institutional frame and therefore carries attributable consequences, even though no prescriptive command is generated.

6.5 Non-innocence note

The scene becomes one of non-innocence not because a hidden duty has been derived, but because omission becomes structurally traceable once access, asymmetry, temporality, and self-binding are in place.

6.6 Hume-boundary note

Nothing in this example derives an ought from an is. The reconstruction remains on the side of attributable consequence rather than obligation.

7) Logic Boundary Management

7.1 Role of logic

Logic functions as an ordering instrument for coordinating information, reporting channels, retaliation risk, institutional procedure, and time pressure.

7.2 Limit of logic

Logic cannot guarantee safety, fairness, or justice outcomes, and it cannot transform a coherent reporting analysis into final moral closure.

7.3 Persistent non-closure

Non-closure (Λ) remains within the frame: harm may persist, retaliation may remain unresolved, and no institutional rule fully completes the scene.

7.4 Closure-pressure

Source: ∇ → Σ-totalization risk

Pressure arises to declare whistleblowing universally mandatory, or conversely universally impossible. PMS–LOGIC treats both moves as closure-errors that exceed the legitimate role of logic in the scene.

7.5 Disciplined stopping point

The appropriate endpoint is a structurally disciplined stopping point for logic as used here: coherent coordination without final authority.

8) Typical Drift Markers

  • Dogmatism (Σ-totalization): Treating disclosure as an absolute duty regardless of channel availability, retaliation exposure, or effective capacity.
  • Moralism (pseudo-ought injection): Translating attributable silence into a universal command to disclose.
  • Nihilism (Λ-suppression): Claiming silence is neutral because outcomes were uncertain or because no guaranteed safe path existed.
  • Authority capture (Ω-inflation): Letting institutional power define truth, innocence, or responsibility solely by its own procedural dominance.
  • Technocratic reduction (□-overconstraint): Assuming existing channels exhaust all structural possibilities and dismissing unmodeled remainder or blocked channels as irrelevant.

9) Failure-Mode Constraint Reference

  • FMC2 — Forced closure where Non-Event (Λ: structured absence) persists produces structural instability.
    Silence and unresolved harm remain structurally present. Any attempt to declare the case fully resolved by policy language or moral verdict substitutes false closure for persistent remainder.
  • FMC3 — When explanatory power is converted into sense-making authority, structural overreach occurs.
    Institutional procedure or investigative logic can explain parts of the case without thereby acquiring final authority over meaning, responsibility, or legitimacy.
  • FMC4 — Where proximity generates power, viable praxis requires maintained distance.
    Organizations and insiders operate under asymmetric proximity to information, channels, and consequences. Maintained distance prevents fusion of institutional power with moral authority.
  • FMC5 — Within PMS–LOGIC, stable self-binding takes the form of restraint rather than assertion.
    The example requires restraint against turning attribution into praise, blame, or universal command. Stable self-binding preserves structural accountability without rhetorical escalation.

12) Readable Structural Closure

In the whistleblower case, the appearance of moral pressure does not originate in a hidden duty to report. It arises because information becomes structurally consequential once it is held within a role-defined frame (□), under asymmetrical power relations (Ω), and across irreversible time (Θ). Silence is no longer a neutral absence; it becomes a non-event (Λ) that shapes outcome trajectories. PMS–LOGIC therefore reconstructs the scene as one of attributable omission rather than obligation. Logic can order the scene, but it cannot close it; the remainder must be carried rather than converted into moral finality.

13) Extended Structural Closure

13.1

The whistleblower case sharpens the PMS–LOGIC thesis because it locates responsibility at the point where information, role, and omission become structurally intertwined. The relevant question is not whether a hidden moral law commands disclosure, but whether silence becomes attributable once an actor possesses effective, frame-bound access to channels within a live temporal window. This makes the case especially useful for distinguishing responsibility from ought.

13.2

The decisive boundary is again effective capacity. PMS–LOGIC does not treat “could have spoken in principle” as responsibility-generating. Responsibility tracks what was structurally available in the operative frame: means, access, authority, timing, and exposure conditions. This prevents moral inflation (“you were obliged to sacrifice yourself”) and prevents moral evasion (“nothing belonged to anyone because the system was broken”). Calibration sensitivity remains central where channels are ambiguous, blocked, retaliatory, or temporally degraded.

13.3

What makes the case particularly instructive is the role of Λ under institutional frames. Silence is not an empty gap but a stabilizing absence that interacts with attractors such as normalized delay, deferral, and diffusion of responsibility. Under temporality (Θ), these patterns can compound harm. Moral readability emerges post hoc because consequences become traceable to a structurally sustained omission, not because a norm has been violated.

13.4

Logic’s temptation in this scene is binary closure: either whistleblowing is declared universally mandatory, or it is declared structurally impossible. Both moves exceed logic’s legitimate role. PMS–LOGIC insists that Λ be carried: the frame cannot guarantee safety, justice, or fairness, and this non-closure cannot be repaired by universalized rule language or blanket exemption. Integration (Σ) is used only to coordinate risk, channels, and timing—not to produce a final verdict.

13.5

Distance (Χ) is critical to avoid moral theater. Without Χ, attribution collapses into praise or blame, and self-binding (Ψ) is replaced by external pressure. With Χ, self-binding remains what it is in PMS–LOGIC: attribution-stability across time rather than moral elevation. The employee remains a structurally identifiable bearer of consequences where capacity existed, without being transformed into hero, traitor, or moral exemplar.

13.6

The example therefore exhibits the full PMS–LOGIC architecture: pre-moral structure (knowledge, roles, channels, delay attractors), logical boundary discipline (coordination without closure), and post-moral readability (attributable omission under Ω, Θ, Λ, and Ψ). The result is a structurally sharp but non-prescriptive account: no code, no sanctification, no condemnation, and no hidden ought—only a disciplined reconstruction of how silence becomes legible as responsibility and where attribution must stop.

14) Counter-Reading Relevance

  • D6 — “This cannot guide action.”
    The example strongly tempts readers to extract decision rules; PMS–LOGIC must remain orienting rather than instructive.
  • D7 — “This wins by translation, not necessarily by final superiority.”
    The reconstruction may look compelling because it translates courage, duty, and silence into structural grammar; that does not by itself prove final superiority over rival accounts.
  • D8 — “Relation to adjacent frameworks.”
    The case sits close to deontic theory, virtue ethics, institutional ethics, and political critique; PMS–LOGIC must therefore mark its own reconstructive scope carefully.
