1) Minimal Vignette

1.1 Scene

An adult family member provides ongoing informal care to an elderly relative over an extended period. Care includes coordination, presence, appointments, practical support, and intermittent crisis response, while professional services remain partial, delayed, or unevenly accessible.

1.2 Stakes

The scene affects health outcomes, dependency trajectories, caregiver exhaustion, distribution of family burden, and long-term life reorganization for all involved.

1.3 Structural issue

The issue is how responsibility becomes attributable through asymmetry and time in an everyday care trajectory without being translated into command, total obligation, or idealized family morality.

2) Why This Is Repo-Useful

2.1 Structural focus

This example is useful because it shows PMS–LOGIC in an ordinary but highly pressure-loaded scene where responsibility appears almost automatically moralized. It concentrates vulnerability, continuity, fatigue, omission, and capacity boundaries in a way that makes the responsibility/ought distinction especially clear.

2.2 Lens relevance

It demonstrates how moral readability emerges in sustained care without requiring hidden norm derivation. It also shows why family relations are especially vulnerable to pseudo-ought injection, guilt escalation, and authority capture through vulnerability claims.

2.3 Repository value

As a test vector, the case helps distinguish PMS–LOGIC from moralizing care ethics, total-duty family narratives, and nihilistic retreat. It shows how care becomes structurally attributable while remaining bounded by actual, sustainable capacity.

3) Frame Validity

3.1 Operative frame — □

  • Role space:
    • Primary informal caregiver
    • Care recipient with varying dependency and vulnerability
    • Extended family with uneven participation or absence
    • Healthcare, homecare, and social services with partial availability
  • Scope boundaries:
    • Informal care capacity is limited by time, health, access, finances, mobility, and support systems.
    • The operative frame includes actual service availability, transport, scheduling, fatigue limits, and institutional interfaces.
    • Only what can be sustained within real capacity and time windows counts as operative possibility.
  • Out of frame:
    • Idealized family norms
    • Heroism narratives
    • Total obligation
    • Retrospective purity tests
    • Any assumption that kinship as such produces unlimited attributable capacity

Frame integrity note: The example remains valid only so long as actual supports, absences, and limits are represented as part of the frame. If cultural expectations replace operative capacity analysis, the scene becomes morally inflated and structurally blurred.

4.4 Structural analysis by operator

  • Δ (Difference):
    • Available vs. unavailable support
    • Immediate care vs. deferred care
    • Sustainable response vs. overextension
    • Within-capacity assistance vs. beyond-capacity expectation
    • Shared care vs. default concentration on one caregiver
  • ∇ (Impulse):
    • Drive to preserve care continuity despite growing strain
    • Drive to close gaps by personal overextension
    • Drive to find “the right balance” between self-preservation and care
    • Drive to compensate structural insufficiency through informal sacrifice
  • □ (Frame):
    • Family relationship and recurring care arrangement
    • Actual access to services, appointments, respite, transport, and support
    • Time availability, health constraints, income constraints, and logistical feasibility
    • Cultural expectations only insofar as they actually shape operative pressures
    • Institutional and service interfaces that affect what care is possible
  • Λ (Non-Event):
    • Gaps in service provision
    • Missed interventions due to fatigue, overload, or time scarcity
    • Delayed appointments, follow-up, or coordination
    • Absence of sufficient shared support
    • No allocation of effort resolves all needs or prevents all loss
  • Α (Attractor):
    • Routine reliance on the same caregiver
    • Normalization of partial care and chronic insufficiency
    • Habitual deferral by others to the caregiver already carrying the load
    • Stabilized expectation that the existing care trajectory will continue
  • Ω (Asymmetry):
    • Exposure gradients:
      • The care recipient is highly vulnerable to interruption, delay, or partial care
      • The caregiver typically has greater operative capacity, but also asymmetric exposure to burnout and cumulative burden
      • Extended family and institutions may be less immediately exposed to consequences than either caregiver or recipient
    • Capacity gradients:
      • Care capacity is finite, role-bound, and time-bound
      • The caregiver often has more immediate ability to affect outcomes than others in the frame
      • Capacity is unequal not only between caregiver and recipient, but also across the wider family and service network

    Note: Ω here marks structural imbalance in vulnerability, access, and ability to intervene. It does not by itself produce duty or moral command.

  • Θ (Temporality):
    • Health declines accumulate
    • Missed care has lasting effects
    • Fatigue and overload compound over time
    • Care trajectories do not reset after one missed intervention or one exhausted period
    • Delayed support can foreclose later possibilities
  • Φ (Recontextualization):
    • Shift from “total care” to “sustainable care within capacity.”
    • Shift from “family duty” to “capacity-bounded care trajectory under asymmetry.”
    • Shift from solitary care expectation to distributed support where feasible.
  • Χ (Distance):
    • Restrain moral self-judgment and heroization
    • Prevent vulnerability from becoming command without capacity checks
    • Keep the analysis structural rather than sanctifying sacrifice or condemning limitation
    • Hold open the difference between attributable consequence and unconditional duty
  • Σ (Integration):
    • Coordinate care tasks, service access, fatigue limits, and temporal urgency
    • Preserve coherence in a livable care pattern without claiming completeness
    • Integrate supports and boundaries without pretending that all unmet needs can be eliminated
  • Ψ (Self-Binding):
    • Continuity of commitment stabilizes attribution over time
    • The caregiving trajectory remains identifiable across repeated acts, omissions, and revisions
    • Responsibility persists where capacity and continuity persist, without becoming prescription

5) Effective Capacity Boundary

Boundary condition reference: BC_EFFECTIVE_CAPACITY

Statement: Responsibility presupposes effective, sustainable, frame-bound care capacity: actual time, access, practical ability, health, support, and open temporal windows within which action can still matter.

No capacity, no attribution: Where caregiving capacity collapses through exhaustion, service absence, health limitation, loss of access, or closed time windows, attribution does not simply continue by moral expectation alone.

Calibration note: The threshold at which care capacity becomes effectively attributable remains calibration-sensitive. Borderline cases may include chronic fatigue, fluctuating health, partial service access, unstable support from others, and ambiguous temporal urgency. PMS–LOGIC specifies structural form, not every sufficiency threshold in advance.

Anti-inflation note: This boundary prevents both moral inflation (“as family, one must always do more”) and moral erasure (“because not everything can be done, nothing belongs to anyone”).

6) Moral Readability Without “Ought”

6.1 Status

Post-moral readability

6.2 Minimal operator basis

Ω + Θ + Λ + Ψ

6.3 Emergence statement

Moral readability emerges post hoc because ongoing care unfolds under vulnerability, unequal capacity, durable effects, structured absences, and continuity of commitment.

6.4 Omission non-neutrality

Non-provision, delay, missed intervention, or partial care function as non-events (Λ) within the care frame and therefore shape outcomes rather than merely failing to add positive action.

6.5 Non-innocence note

The scene becomes one of structural non-innocence not because the caregiver violates a hidden command, but because consequences attach to an ongoing care trajectory wherever effective capacity persists.

6.6 Hume-boundary note

Nothing in this example derives an ought from an is. The reconstruction remains on the side of attributable consequence rather than obligation.

7) Logic Boundary Management

7.1 Role of logic

Logic functions here as an ordering instrument for coordinating needs, finite capacity, temporal urgency, service options, and support limits.

7.2 Limit of logic

No calculation can produce a final, exhaustive, or universally valid right amount of care. Needs exceed capacity, and remainder persists.

7.3 Persistent non-closure

Non-closure (Λ) remains within the frame: unmet needs, fatigue, service gaps, and partial loss cannot be fully eliminated or morally neutralized.

7.4 Closure-pressure

Source: ∇ → Σ-totalization risk

Pressure arises to declare one care pattern, sacrifice level, or balance as finally right, either through self-judgment, family expectation, or simplified moral narratives.

7.5 Disciplined stopping point

The appropriate endpoint is a structurally disciplined stopping point for logic as used here: coordinated care within capacity, without conversion into total duty or final justification.

8) Typical Drift Markers

  • Dogmatism (Σ-totalization): Treating one care pattern or one sacrifice level as exhaustive, final, or fully justified for all future cases.
  • Moralism (pseudo-ought injection): Translating attributable care responsibility into unconditional family duty or endless command.
  • Nihilism (Λ-suppression): Claiming that because limits exist and not everything can be done, responsibility disappears.
  • Authority capture (Ω-inflation): Converting vulnerability or family expectation into command without checking actual capacity.
  • Technocratic reduction (□-overconstraint): Treating the immediate family frame as exhaustive while ignoring alternative supports, services, or redistributions outside the default arrangement.

9) Failure-Mode Constraint Reference

  • FMC1 — A boundary that cannot be carried becomes structurally unstable.
    Care arrangements that exceed sustainable capacity become destructive rather than orienting. Overclaimed care boundaries eventually destabilize both care quality and caregiver viability.
  • FMC2 — Forced closure where Non-Event (Λ: structured absence) persists produces structural instability.
    No care arrangement eliminates all unmet need, fatigue, or loss. Any attempt to declare the remainder resolved replaces structural openness with false certainty or guilt narratives.
  • FMC4 — Where proximity generates power, viable praxis requires maintained distance.
    Care scenes involve extreme proximity, dependency, and influence. Maintained distance is needed so that vulnerability does not become command and care does not become fused with total self-erasure.
  • FMC5 — Within PMS–LOGIC, stable self-binding takes the form of restraint rather than assertion.
    Stable care requires restraint against claims of total obligation, total innocence, or final justification. Self-binding remains continuity of responsibility, not assertion of moral completeness.

12) Readable Structural Closure

The family care case makes visible one of the most persistent confusions PMS–LOGIC is designed to resist: the slide from structural responsibility into implicit obligation. Moral pressure appears here not because a family norm commands care, but because asymmetry (Ω: structural imbalance) and irreversible time (Θ: temporality) render care and non-care consequential once a person occupies a position of effective, ongoing capacity within a shared frame (□). PMS–LOGIC therefore reconstructs the scene as one of attributable consequence under continuity, not as one of derived duty. Logic may coordinate the care scene, but it cannot close it; the remainder must be carried rather than converted into command or guilt.

13) Extended Structural Closure

13.1

The family care example is especially instructive because it is both ordinary and structurally intense. It shows how moral language arises in everyday life without requiring a hidden normative engine. Care becomes readable because health trajectories, dependency relations, and missed interventions accumulate over time. The scene is already asymmetrical and temporally charged before anyone starts talking in the language of duty.

13.2

The key mechanism is continuity under asymmetry. As long as effective care capacity exists, however limited, actions and omissions shape outcomes that cannot be reset. Missed appointments, delayed responses, periods of exhaustion, and partial support are not empty gaps. They are non-events (Λ) within an established dependency frame, and therefore participate in consequence formation. Responsibility becomes readable because consequences attach to a caregiving trajectory, not because a hidden command has been violated.

13.3

The decisive safeguard is the effective-capacity boundary. PMS–LOGIC does not treat kinship as such as responsibility-generating. Responsibility tracks effective, sustainable capacity within time, not abstract family expectation or moral mythology. This blocks both dominant distortions: moralism (“you must always do more because you are family”) and nihilism (“because you cannot do everything, nothing belongs to anyone”). Capacity remains the condition of attribution.

13.4

The case also reveals the limit of logic. Care scenes invite calculation: how much is enough, what is fair, what is the right balance? But no final balance is structurally available here. Needs exceed capacity, losses remain, and remainder persists. Closure-pressure arises as guilt narratives, family pressure, or self-judgment, each pushing integration (Σ) toward totalization. PMS–LOGIC resists this move by insisting that Λ be carried rather than resolved.

13.5

Distance (Χ) is therefore not withdrawal from care but protection against escalation. It prevents vulnerability from becoming command and prevents responsibility from collapsing into self-accusation. Integration (Σ) coordinates care tasks, limits, and support systems into a livable pattern without claiming completeness. Recontextualization (Φ) then becomes possible: care can shift through new services, redistributed support, or changed roles without erasing prior consequences.

13.6

Self-binding (Ψ) stabilizes attribution without turning it into prescription. The caregiver remains a continuous bearer of consequences across time not because they ought to be, but because the care trajectory is theirs insofar as capacity and continuity persist. This is responsibility as non-innocence, not command. The example therefore displays the full PMS–LOGIC architecture: pre-moral structure (dependency, gaps, routines), logical boundary discipline (coordination without closure), and post-moral readability (attributable consequences under Ω, Θ, Λ, and Ψ).
